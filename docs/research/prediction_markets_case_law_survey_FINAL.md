## 1. Executive Summary

The prediction markets industry faces the most significant legal reckoning in its history. As of June 17, 2026, the landscape is defined by:

- **One federal appellate ruling** (Third Circuit, April 6, 2026): Rules for prediction markets — sports event contracts are CEA "swaps"; CEA field- and conflict-preempts state gambling laws as applied to CFTC-regulated Designated Contract Markets (DCMs).
- **Three federal appeals pending** (Ninth Circuit, Fourth Circuit, Sixth Circuit): The Ninth and Fourth Circuit panels have signaled skepticism toward platforms; a circuit split with the Third Circuit is widely expected.
- **Eight states sued by the CFTC** in an unprecedented offensive campaign (Arizona, Connecticut, Illinois, New York, Wisconsin, Minnesota, Rhode Island, New Mexico).
- **Two bellwether pending decisions**: Massachusetts SJC ruling (expected late June/July 2026) and Ninth Circuit ruling (expected July–August 2026).
- **Supreme Court certiorari probability at 64%** by year-end 2026, per Polymarket's own prediction markets.
- **Nevada and Massachusetts** — the two states the Ifrah Law baseline article omitted — have the most developed enforcement timelines of any state, with preliminary injunctions issued and state supreme court review underway.
- **Eighteen states** are in active legal disputes with prediction market platforms. A 41-state AG coalition has submitted comments to the CFTC arguing for state authority.
- **Insider trading** has emerged as a serious parallel enforcement track: the first prediction market insider trading case (CFTC v. Van Dyke) and a second follow-on case (U.S. v. Spagnuolo) are both active.
- **CFTC rulemaking** advanced significantly: an ANPR was published March 12, 2026, and a full NPRM (267 pages) was published June 10, 2026, with comment deadline July 27, 2026.

## 2. Overview: The Legal Landscape

### The Core Legal Dispute

The central legal question is whether the Commodity Exchange Act (CEA), 7 U.S.C. § 2(a)(1)(A), as amended by the Dodd-Frank Act (2010), grants the CFTC **exclusive jurisdiction** over event contracts ("prediction market" contracts) traded on CFTC-licensed Designated Contract Markets (DCMs), thereby preempting state gambling and sports wagering laws.

**Prediction market platforms' position:** Their contracts are "swaps" (as defined in CEA § 1a(47)(A)(ii) — agreements dependent on the occurrence of an event "associated with a potential financial, economic, or commercial consequence") traded on federally regulated DCMs under CFTC's exclusive jurisdiction; state gambling laws are preempted under field preemption, conflict preemption, and/or express preemption.

**States' position:** CEA was enacted to regulate financial derivatives that caused the 2008 financial crisis — not to preempt century-old state police powers over gambling. Sports event contracts are not "swaps" because sports outcomes are not "associated with a potential financial, economic, or commercial consequence" in any meaningful CEA sense. Even if they were swaps, Congress did not clearly intend to strip states of gambling regulation authority. The major-questions doctrine requires congressional clarity that is absent.

### The Platforms

The primary CFTC-licensed DCMs at issue:
- **KalshiEX LLC** (d/b/a Kalshi) — the lead litigant in most cases
- **QCX LLC** (d/b/a Polymarket US) — acquired by Polymarket July 21, 2025 for $112M; CFTC-licensed DCM
- **North American Derivatives Exchange, Inc.** (d/b/a Crypto.com Derivatives North America / NADEX) — owned by Crypto.com; pre-existing CFTC-licensed DCM
- **Robinhood Derivatives, LLC** — CFTC-registered futures commission merchant (FCM), accesses event contracts through Kalshi's and ForecastEx's DCMs
- **Coinbase Financial Markets, Inc.** — accessed Kalshi's DCM; launched independent prediction markets in January 2026

### CFTC Leadership

- **Chairman:** Michael S. Selig (Trump appointee) — aggressively asserting CFTC exclusive jurisdiction; has personally stated "we will see you in court" to state regulators and filed eight state lawsuits since April 2026.

## 3. Federal Baseline Cases

### 3A. KalshiEX LLC v. CFTC — Congressional Control Contracts

**Dockets:** D.D.C. No. 1:23-cv-03257-JMC; D.C. Cir. No. 24-5205  
**Judge:** Hon. Jia M. Cobb (D.D.C.); Panel: Millett, Pillard, Pan (D.C. Cir.)

**Background:** KalshiEX sought to list "Congressional Control Contracts" (CCCs) — binary event contracts on which party controls the U.S. Senate/House. The CFTC prohibited the CCCs in September 2023 under CEA § 7a-2(c)(5)(C)(i) (the "special rule"), finding they involved "gaming" and activity "unlawful under state law" and were contrary to the public interest.

#### D.D.C. — September 6/12, 2024

Judge Cobb issued a bench order on September 6, 2024, and a formal Memorandum Opinion on September 12, 2024.

**Holding:** The CCCs do not involve "gaming" or "unlawful activity" within § 7a-2(c)(5)(C)(i). The special rule was never triggered; the court did not reach the "contrary to the public interest" prong.

**Key reasoning:**
- "Gaming," as used in the special rule, refers to "playing games or playing games for stakes." The CCCs involve elections and politics — not any game.
- The court rejected an interpretation of "gaming" so broad it would subject all event contracts to the special rule, defeating the enumerated structure of § 7a-2(c)(5)(C)(i).
- Conclusion: "The Court concludes that Kalshi's congressional control contracts do not involve unlawful activity or gaming. Accordingly, the Court must GRANT Plaintiff's Motion for Summary Judgment and DENY Defendant's Cross-Motion for Summary Judgment."

**Source:** [D.D.C. Memorandum Opinion (DWT)](https://www.dwt.com/-/media/files/blogs/financial-services-law-advisor/2024/10/cftccongressgamblingorder.pdf)

#### D.C. Circuit Stay Denial — October 2, 2024

The D.C. Circuit granted an administrative stay on September 12, 2024, then denied the CFTC's motion for a stay pending appeal on October 2, 2024. Panel (Millett, J.): "Because the Commission has failed at this time to demonstrate that it or the public will be irreparably injured absent a stay, we deny its motion without prejudice."

**Source:** [D.C. Circuit Opinion (October 2, 2024)](https://media.cadc.uscourts.gov/opinions/docs/2024/10/24-5205-2077790.pdf)

#### CFTC Voluntary Dismissal — May 5, 2025

The CFTC voted 3-0 (one abstention) to abandon the appeal. Document #2114321, Filed May 5, 2025. The dismissal left the D.D.C. summary judgment intact: each party bears its own costs; Kalshi waived EAJA claims.

**Significance:** Established (without D.C. Circuit binding precedent) that the CFTC exceeded its authority under § 7a-2(c)(5)(C)(i) in prohibiting CCCs. Election contracts now trade freely on Kalshi.

**Source:** [CFTC Voluntary Dismissal Motion (CCH)](https://business.cch.com/srd/20250505_KalshiEx-v-CFTC_motion_CFTC.pdf)

### 3B. Clarke v. CFTC — PredictIt

**Dockets:** 5th Cir. No. 22-51124 → **74 F.4th 627 (5th Cir. 2023)** [VERIFIED]; W.D. Tex. No. 1:24-cv-00614-DAE  
**Judge (District):** Hon. David A. Ezra (W.D. Tex.)

**Background:** Victoria University of Wellington, New Zealand, operated PredictIt under CFTC No-Action Letter 14-130. In August 2022, the CFTC withdrew the no-action letter (via Letters 22-08 and 23-03) with little explanation, effectively closing the market. Victoria University affiliates and PredictIt users sued.

#### Fifth Circuit — 74 F.4th 627 (5th Cir. 2023)

In a 2-1 opinion, the Fifth Circuit held:
1. The CFTC's withdrawal of No-Action Letter 14-130 constituted **final agency action** subject to APA review (reversing prior circuit precedent).
2. Plaintiffs had standing.
3. The rescission was **likely arbitrary and capricious**.
4. Remanded with instructions to enter a preliminary injunction; enjoined the CFTC from closing PredictIt until 60 days after final judgment.

**Source:** [5th Cir. opinion (uscourts.gov)](https://www.ca5.uscourts.gov/opinions/pub/22/22-51124-CV0.pdf)

#### CFTC Staff Letter 25-20 — July 14, 2025

The CFTC issued Staff Letter 25-20, amending Letter 14-130. Key changes:
- **Operator:** Transfers operation to Prediction Market Research Consortium, Inc. (PMRC), a U.S.-based 501(c)(3) applicant
- **Trader cap:** Eliminated (was 5,000 per contract)
- **Investment limit:** Raised from $850 to $3,500 (current FECA individual contribution limit; indexed going forward)

**Source:** [CFTC Letter 25-20](https://www.cftc.gov/csl/25-20/download)

#### W.D. Tex. Final Judgment — July 22, 2025

Judge Ezra entered final judgment:
1. **Vacated** CFTC's withdrawal of no-action relief as "arbitrary, capricious, and contrary to law."
2. **Declared** the CFTC's stated justifications legally insufficient.
3. **Permanently enjoined** the CFTC from closing PredictIt based on facts known as of July 22, 2024.

**Source:** [VitalLaw/CCH Summary (July 23, 2025)](https://business.cch.com/srd/EXCHANGESANDMARKETREGULATION%E2%80%94WDTexCourtvacatesCFTCwithdrawalofno-actionreliefenjoinsPredictItmarketshutdown(Jul232025).pdf); [PredictIt press release (July 23, 2025)](https://www.globenewswire.com/news-release/2025/07/23/3120660/0/en/PredictIt-Announces-a-Resolution-to-Litigation-with-the-CFTC-and-a-Bright-Future-Ahead.html)

## 4. Third Circuit: First Federal Appellate Preemption Ruling

### KalshiEX LLC v. Flaherty — **172 F.4th 220 (3d Cir. 2026)** [VERIFIED]

**Dockets:** D.N.J. No. 1:25-cv-02152-ESK-MJS; 3d Cir. No. 25-1922  
**Decided:** April 6, 2026 (argued September 10, 2025)  
**Panel:** Porter, J. (majority, joined by Chagares, C.J.); Roth, J. (dissenting)

This is the **first and only federal appellate court ruling on the core prediction market preemption question** as of June 17, 2026.

#### Background

New Jersey filed suit alleging Kalshi's sports event contracts violated NJ gambling laws and the NJ Constitution's prohibition on gambling without legislative authorization. The District of New Jersey denied Kalshi's motion to dismiss. Kalshi sought an emergency preliminary injunction.

#### Holdings

**(a) Sports event contracts are "swaps" under CEA § 1a(47)(A)(ii):**

> "Because Kalshi's sports-related event contracts are traded on a CFTC-licensed DCM and depend on event outcomes associated with economic consequences, they fit within the Act's definition of 'swaps' subject to the CFTC's jurisdiction."

The court rejected New Jersey's requirement that a sports outcome be "joined or connected" with "a financial, economic, or commercial instrument or measure," finding that standard "raises the bar beyond what the Act requires." The court pointed to sponsors, advertisers, television networks, franchises, and local/national communities as evidence of economic consequences.

**(b) CEA field-preempts state gambling law:**

> "We agree. Kalshi has demonstrated a reasonable chance of success in showing that the text of the Act preempts otherwise applicable state laws that purport to regulate sports-related event contracts on CFTC-licensed DCMs."

The field is "the regulation of trading on a DCM" — not the broader field of gambling generally.

**(c) CEA conflict-preempts state gambling law:**

> "Conflict preemption also prohibits New Jersey from regulating sports-related event contracts on CFTC-licensed DCMs... Allowing New Jersey to enforce its gambling laws and state constitution would create an obstacle to executing the Act because such state enforcement would prohibit Kalshi, which operates a licensed DCM under the exclusive jurisdiction of the CFTC, from offering its sports-related event contracts in New Jersey."

**(d) Presumption against preemption overcome:**

The majority acknowledged the presumption but held it was overcome by the Act's text: "Even accepting the dissent's premise that states have long regulated gambling, the text preempts state gambling laws that seek to regulate futures trading, i.e., Kalshi's sports-related event contracts traded on a DCM under the exclusive jurisdiction of the CFTC."

The majority cited *Churchill Downs Tech. Initiatives Co. v. Mich. Gaming Control Bd.*, 162 F.4th 631, 641 n.5 (6th Cir. 2025): "since the regulation of interstate gambling isn't a traditional area of state regulation, the presumption against preemption doesn't apply."

#### Dissent (Roth, J.)

Judge Roth argued the presumption against preemption applies "with special force" in gambling, invoking *Altria Grp., Inc. v. Good*, 555 U.S. 70, 77 (2008); *Rice v. Santa Fe Elevator Corp.*, 331 U.S. 218, 230 (1947). She further argued CFTC Rule 40.11 — which itself prohibits DCMs from listing gaming contracts — undercuts the conflict preemption holding.

#### Post-Decision Status

- **No en banc petition filed** as of June 17, 2026.
- **New Jersey has announced intent to petition for certiorari.** Per the May 19, 2026 joint status report in D.N.J. (Document 41): "Defendants have indicated that they intend to seek further review of the Third Circuit's decision in the U.S. Supreme Court." Deadline: approximately early July 2026.
- D.N.J. case stayed pending SCOTUS action.

**Sources:** [Third Circuit Opinion (ca3.uscourts.gov)](https://www2.ca3.uscourts.gov/opinarch/251922p.pdf); [D.N.J. Joint Status Report May 19, 2026 (DeFiRate)](https://defirate.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/05/kalshilflahertystay.pdf); [Holland & Knight alert (April 7, 2026)](https://www.hklaw.com/en/insights/publications/2026/04/federal-appeals-court-cftc-jurisdiction-over-sports-event-contracts); [Lowenstein Sandler alert (April 20, 2026)](https://www.lowenstein.com/news-insights/publications/client-alerts/third-circuit-affirms-preliminary-injunction-in-favor-of-kalshi-fctm)

### 5.2 Kalshi v. Hendrick — Federal Court (D. Nev. No. 2:25-cv-00575-APG-BNW)

**Judge:** Chief Judge Andrew P. Gordon

#### April 9, 2025 — Preliminary Injunction GRANTED (Hendrick I)

**Citation:** *KalshiEX LLC v. Hendrick*, 2025 WL 1073495 (D. Nev. Apr. 9, 2025)

The court enjoined the NGCB and Nevada Gaming Commission from enforcing preempted state laws against Kalshi and from pursuing civil or criminal prosecutions against Kalshi for offering event-based contracts on a CFTC-designated market. Applying the *Winter* test:

1. **Likelihood of success:** Kalshi demonstrated a likelihood that the CEA preempts Nevada gaming laws.
2. **Irreparable harm:** Monetary harm irreparable because the Tenth Amendment bars recovery of damages against state agencies/officials.
3. **Balance of hardships:** Favored Kalshi.
4. **Public interest:** Favored injunction.

Kalshi required to post $10,000 bond.

**Source:** [PI Grant Order (Justia)](https://cases.justia.com/federal/district-courts/nevada/nvdce/2:2025cv00575/173903/45/0.pdf?ts=1744279448)

#### November 24, 2025 — Preliminary Injunction DISSOLVED (Hendrick II)

**Citation:** *KalshiEX LLC v. Hendrick*, 2025 WL 3286282 (D. Nev. Nov. 24, 2025)

In a 29-page opinion, Chief Judge Gordon granted the NGCB's motion to dissolve the preliminary injunction because "the law and facts have evolved in this court and others."

**Key holding:** Kalshi's sports-gaming contracts are **not swaps** — the phrase "associated with a potential financial, economic, or commercial consequence" in CEA § 1a(47)(A)(ii) **requires the underlying event or contingency to be inherently associated with a financial, economic, or commercial consequence**, not merely that the event may have some potential downstream financial consequences. A sporting event outcome is not inherently associated with commercial consequences.

This ruling came within days of the same court's October 14, 2025 denial of a PI in the Crypto.com case (No. 2:25-CV-00978-APG-BNW, 2025 WL 2916151).

- Kalshi appealed to the Ninth Circuit (No. 25-7516), filed November 25, 2025.
- D. Nev. denied injunction pending appeal, December 16, 2025 (2025 WL 3642346).

**Source:** [PI Dissolution (CCH)](https://business.cch.com/srd/20251201_SRD_NV_Kalshi-v-Hendrick_order_injunction_dissolved.pdf)

### 5.5 Coinbase v. Ford (D. Nev. No. 2:26-cv-00256-CDS-MDC) — Younger Abstention

**Judge:** Chief Judge Cristina D. Silva  
**Ruling:** February 7, 2026 — **Case dismissed**

Coinbase filed in federal court the day before its Nevada state-court TRO (No. 26 OC 00030 1B) was granted, seeking to preemptively enjoin Nevada enforcement. The court dismissed on two independent grounds:

1. **Younger abstention:** All three elements met — (i) ongoing state enforcement; (ii) important state interests (regulation of gaming, protection of the public); (iii) adequate opportunity to litigate federal claims in state court.
2. **Anti-Injunction Act** (28 U.S.C. § 2283): "An absolute bar to any federal court action that has the effect of staying a pending state court proceeding." No exceptions applied.

**Nevada state court follow-up:** Judge Kristin Luis (First Judicial District) issued a preliminary injunction ~March 30, 2026 barring Coinbase from offering sports, election, and entertainment wagering in Nevada; gave Coinbase 60 days to implement geofencing compliance.

## 6. Massachusetts: Complete Enforcement Timeline

## 7. CFTC Offensive State Litigation (Eight States)

Under CFTC Chairman Michael S. Selig, the CFTC launched an unprecedented campaign of affirmative federal litigation beginning April 2, 2026. As of June 17, 2026, the CFTC has sued **eight states**.

**Legal theory in every case:** CEA § 2(a)(1)(A) grants the CFTC "exclusive jurisdiction" over swaps and futures traded on federally regulated exchanges; state gambling laws are preempted — expressly, by field, by obstacle, and/or by impossibility — as applied to those DCMs.

### 7.1 Arizona — *United States v. Arizona*, No. 26-cv-02246 (D. Ariz.)

**Consolidated into:** *KalshiEX LLC v. Jackie Johnson*, No. CV-26-01715-PHX-MTL  
**Judge:** Michael T. Liburdi (Trump appointee)

**State trigger:** On March 17, 2026, Arizona AG Kris Mayes filed **20 misdemeanor criminal charges** against KalshiEX LLC and Kalshi Trading LLC in Maricopa County Superior Court — the first criminal prosecution ever brought against a CFTC-registered prediction market operator:
- 4 counts: Election wagering (2028 presidential race, 2026 AZ gubernatorial race, 2026 AZ Republican gubernatorial primary, 2026 AZ Secretary of State race, SAVE Act becoming law)
- 16 counts: Unlawful betting and wagering (professional and college sports, player proposition bets)

**CFTC's April 10, 2026 TRO:** Judge Liburdi granted, blocking Arizona from proceeding with criminal arraignment of Kalshi (scheduled for April 13). CFTC: "Arizona's decision to weaponize state criminal law against companies that comply with federal law sets a dangerous precedent."

**May 5, 2026 — Preliminary Injunction GRANTED:** *KalshiEX LLC v. Jackie Johnson*, 2026 WL 1223373 (D. Ariz. May 5, 2026). First PI granted by the **federal government** (not just Kalshi itself). Three independent preemption grounds:

1. **Field preemption:** CEA's "exclusive jurisdiction" language denies jurisdiction to any entity besides the CFTC; regulatory framework "so pervasive" that it "left no room for the States to supplement it."
2. **Obstacle (conflict) preemption:** State prosecution "stands as an obstacle" to Congress's purposes — allowing state prosecution would create "the inconsistent regulatory patchwork that Congress intended to avoid."
3. **Impossibility preemption:** CFTC regulations require DCMs to provide "impartial access"; Arizona law criminalizes event wagering without a state license, making simultaneous compliance **impossible**.

The court agreed with the Third Circuit's *Flaherty* (172 F.4th 220) on the swap definition. "Federal law preempts state gambling laws insofar as they seek to regulate derivatives exchanged on markets regulated by the CFTC."

**Sources:** [National Law Review](https://natlawreview.com/article/federal-court-blocks-arizona-enforcing-gambling-laws-against-prediction-markets); [Arizona AG Press Release](https://www.azag.gov/press-release/attorney-general-mayes-charges-kalshi-illegal-gambling-operation-election-wagering)

The Ho-Chunk ruling stands in direct tension with the California district court ruling in *Blue Lake Rancheria* (IGRA does not apply to third-party platforms). The two cases may generate a circuit conflict on the tribal claims track.

## 15. CFTC Rulemaking

### 15.1 Advanced Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (ANPR) — March 12, 2026

**Press Release:** [No. 9194-26 (March 12, 2026)](https://www.cftc.gov/PressRoom/PressReleases/9194-26)  
**Federal Register:** 91 Fed. Reg. 12,516 (Mar. 16, 2026); RIN 3038–AF65  
**Comment deadline:** April 30, 2026 (closed)

Chairman Selig: "Today's action is an important step in the Commission's continued effort to promote responsible innovation in our derivatives markets. This begins the process of new rulemaking grounded in a rational and coherent interpretation of the Commodity Exchange Act, while reassuring the American people that the CFTC will exercise its exclusive jurisdiction over prediction markets."

The ANPR noted that DCM registration applications had "more than doubled over the past year, largely from entities that are interested primarily, or exclusively, in operating prediction markets."

Seven key regulatory questions addressed: Core Principles applicability; prohibited contracts; sports event contracts and the definition of "gaming"; manipulation and asymmetric information; cost-benefit considerations; state preemption; and insider trading measures.

---

### 15.2 Notice of Proposed Rulemaking (NPRM) — June 10, 2026

**Press Release:** [No. 9249-26 (June 10, 2026)](https://www.cftc.gov/PressRoom/PressReleases/9249-26)  
**Federal Register:** Vol. 91, No. 113, June 12, 2026; RIN **3038–AF65**  
**Comment deadline: July 27, 2026**  
**Length:** 267 pages  
**Title:** "Prediction Markets; Public Interest Determinations"

**Key elements of the NPRM:**
- **No categorical ban:** Rule 40.11(a) does not impose a blanket prohibition; establishes a three-step inquiry.
- **Three-step inquiry:** (i) confirm the contract is an event contract; (ii) determine it involves an enumerated activity; (iii) assess whether it is contrary to the public interest.
- **Definition of "gaming":** Any activity that (i) participants engage in for recreation or entertainment, (ii) is governed by rules, and (iii) features measurable outcomes dependent on luck, skill, or athletic ability. **Explicitly excluded:** political elections, Nobel Prizes, Academy Awards (serve non-recreational purposes).
- **Sports contracts framework:** Contracts on aggregate professional/collegiate sports outcomes (final scores, win-loss records, season statistics) with objective criteria and sports-body coordination are generally viewed favorably; contracts tied to discrete in-game events, player injuries, officiating, youth sports, or pure chance are likely contrary to public interest.
- **90-day enhanced procedural review process** with specific deadlines.
- **Effective date (if finalized):** 60 days after Federal Register publication.

Chairman Selig: "The CFTC will protect the integrity of our regulated markets without standing in the way of responsible innovation. This proposal gives the Commission a durable, transparent framework to identify the contracts Congress directed us to scrutinize while letting legitimate markets move forward."

**Sources:** [Milbank analysis (June 11, 2026)](https://www.milbank.com/en/news/cftc-proposes-a-comprehensive-framework-for-prediction-markets-key-takeaways-from-the-notice-of-proposed-rulemaking.html); [JD Supra (June 16, 2026)](https://www.jdsupra.com/legalnews/cftc-issues-prediction-market-notice-of-9693590/)

1. **Massachusetts SJC ruling** (*Commonwealth v. KalshiEX*, No. SJC-13906) — Expected late June or early July 2026. The bellwether state-court decision on whether states can regulate prediction markets. If the SJC sides with Massachusetts, it triggers CFTC enforcement across New England and likely accelerates SCOTUS certiorari.

2. **New Jersey certiorari petition** (*KalshiEX v. Flaherty*, 172 F.4th 220) — Deadline approximately early July 2026. If filed, the D.N.J. case remains stayed; if certiorari is granted, all lower-court proceedings will be significantly affected.

3. **Minnesota PI hearing** (*United States v. Minnesota*, No. 0:26-cv-02661) — July 1, 2026. If no PI issues, Minnesota's felony ban takes effect August 1, 2026.

4. **Connecticut MTD oral argument** (*United States v. Connecticut*, No. 3:26-cv-00498) — July 22, 2026.

5. **Rhode Island TRO hearing** (*KalshiEX v. Furcolo*, No. 1:26-cv-00327) — July 22, 2026.

6. **Blue Lake Rancheria tribal appeal** (No. 25-7504) — Ninth Circuit oral argument July 10, 2026.

7. **CFTC NPRM comment deadline** — July 27, 2026.

8. **Sixth Circuit oral argument** (Ohio No. 26-3196 + Tennessee No. 26-5235, consolidated) — July 30, 2026.

### Near-Term (July–September 2026)

9. **Ninth Circuit ruling** (Nos. 25-7516, 25-7187, 25-7831) — Expected July–August 2026. The most consequential pending decision. Near-consensus among observers that the panel (Nelson, Bade, Lee) will rule for the states. If so, a circuit split with the Third Circuit is formally established. Supreme Court certiorari becomes near-certain.

10. **Fourth Circuit ruling** (*KalshiEX v. Martin*, No. 25-1892) — Expected early July to early September 2026. Panel also appeared skeptical of Kalshi.

### The Road to SCOTUS

As of June 17, 2026, Polymarket's own prediction markets assign a **64% probability** that SCOTUS accepts a sports event contract case by year-end 2026. The conditions are nearly fully ripe:
- Third Circuit ruled for platforms (April 6, 2026)
- Ninth Circuit expected to rule for states (July–August 2026)
- New Jersey has announced intent to seek certiorari on *Flaherty*
- 18 states in active legal disputes; 41 AGs formally oppose CFTC jurisdiction
- CFTC rulemaking proceeding; NPRM open through July 27, 2026

If SCOTUS grants certiorari on the prediction markets preemption question, it would be one of the most significant federalism and administrative law cases of the decade — implicating not just sports betting but the entire architecture of the multi-trillion-dollar U.S. event-contract markets.
