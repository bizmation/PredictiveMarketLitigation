# U.S. Prediction Markets: Comprehensive Case Law & Regulatory Survey
### Through June 17, 2026

***

## Executive Summary

The legal landscape surrounding prediction markets has evolved into one of the most consequential jurisdictional battles in U.S. regulatory history. The core dispute is binary: are event contracts traded on CFTC-registered Designated Contract Markets (DCMs) "swaps" subject to federal exclusive jurisdiction under the Commodity Exchange Act (CEA), or are they "wagers" subject to state gambling laws? As of June 2026, at least sixteen states are in active legal proceedings against prediction market platforms — primarily Kalshi, Polymarket, Coinbase, Gemini, Robinhood, and Crypto.com — while the CFTC has sued six states and filed amicus briefs in multiple federal and state appellate courts to defend its jurisdiction. The Third Circuit has ruled in favor of federal preemption; the Ninth Circuit heard oral arguments in April 2026 and is expected to rule within 60–120 days; the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court has the matter before it; and the U.S. Supreme Court appears the inevitable endpoint.[^1][^2][^3]

The Ifrah Law article surveyed FTC endorsement disclosure obligations vis-à-vis prediction market influencers — a real and pending issue — but omitted the substantial federal and state-court litigation underlying the industry's existence. Most notably, it did not cover the Nevada Gaming Control Board's civil enforcement actions, the Massachusetts Attorney General's September 2025 lawsuit and January 2026 preliminary injunction against Kalshi, or the CFTC's six affirmative lawsuits against states. This report fills those gaps with verified, cited sources.[^4]

## I. Federal Regulatory Background

### A. CFTC Jurisdiction and the Commodity Exchange Act Framework

Under the Commodity Exchange Act (CEA), the CFTC holds exclusive jurisdiction over "swaps" traded on CFTC-registered DCMs. Prediction market operators Kalshi and Polymarket both operate as CFTC-licensed DCMs and argue that their event contracts constitute "swaps" with "potential financial, economic, or commercial consequences," preempting all state regulation under the CEA's express preemption provisions. States argue the contracts are pure wagers — the outcome of a sporting event has no independent financial consequence beyond the bet itself — and that state gambling law remains applicable regardless of CFTC registration.[^5][^6][^7][^1]

### B. Polymarket — CFTC 2022 Settlement (*In re Blockratize, Inc.*, CFTC Docket No. 22-09)

The first major federal action involving a prediction market platform arose when the CFTC settled with Blockratize, Inc. (d/b/a Polymarket) in January 2022. The CFTC found that Polymarket's event-based binary option contracts constituted "swaps" under the CEA and that the company had been operating an unregistered facility for trading such swaps since approximately June 2020. Polymarket paid a $1.4 million civil penalty and agreed to wind down noncompliant contracts without admitting or denying wrongdoing. This settlement is noteworthy because it established that prediction market contracts can be "swaps" under federal law — the same legal theory the CFTC now invokes to preempt state regulation.[^8][^9][^10][^11]

### C. KalshiEX LLC v. CFTC — Congressional Control Contracts (*KalshiEX LLC v. CFTC*, Nos. 1:23-cv-03257 (D.D.C.) and 24-5205 (D.C. Cir. 2024))

In September 2023, the CFTC issued an order disapproving "Congressional Control Contracts" self-certified by Kalshi, concluding the contracts involved "gaming" and activity unlawful under state law, and were contrary to the public interest under CEA Section 5c(c)(5)(C). Kalshi challenged the order under the Administrative Procedure Act. On September 6, 2024, U.S. District Judge Jia Cobb of the District of Columbia ruled for Kalshi, vacating the CFTC's order and holding that Kalshi's election contracts "do not involve unlawful activity or gaming — they involve elections, which are neither". The CFTC sought an emergency stay pending appeal in the D.C. Circuit; the D.C. Circuit unanimously denied the stay because the CFTC failed to demonstrate a likelihood of irreparable harm. The CFTC ultimately dismissed its appeal, leaving the district court ruling in favor of Kalshi as final precedent. This case is critical because it established that (a) election-related contracts are not inherently "gaming" under federal law; and (b) the CFTC cannot unilaterally ban lawful event contracts by stretching the public-interest exception.[^12][^13][^14][^15]

### D. CFTC/DOJ Endorsement Actions Against States — Overview

Following a wave of state cease-and-desist orders beginning in early 2025 and accelerating into 2026, the CFTC launched an affirmative litigation campaign, filing six direct lawsuits against states and amicus briefs in multiple appellate proceedings to defend its exclusive jurisdiction. The legal theory is consistent across all CFTC filings: the CEA's exclusive-jurisdiction grant preempts state gambling statutes as applied to event contracts traded on CFTC-registered DCMs.[^16][^17][^18][^1]

## II. Federal Court Decisions on Preemption

### A. *KalshiEx LLC v. New Jersey Division of Gaming Enforcement* — Third Circuit (First Federal Appellate Ruling)
**Case No.: 25-1922 (3d Cir. Apr. 6, 2026)**

The Third Circuit issued the first federal appellate decision on whether sports event contracts traded on a CFTC-licensed DCM qualify as swaps subject to CFTC exclusive jurisdiction. In a 2-1 decision authored by Judge David Porter and joined by Chief Judge Michael Chagares, the panel affirmed the District of New Jersey's preliminary injunction blocking New Jersey from applying its gambling laws to Kalshi.[^6][^7]

The majority held that sports event contracts fit within the CEA's definition of "swaps" because they have potential "financial, economic, or commercial consequences" — acknowledging downstream commercial effects such as merchandise sales, stadium revenue, and related market impacts. The panel found both field preemption (the CEA grants the CFTC exclusive jurisdiction over swaps traded on a DCM, leaving no room for state regulation) and conflict preemption (it is impossible for Kalshi to simultaneously comply with state gambling license requirements and operate as a CFTC-registered DCM). The dissent would have held that Congress did not intend to preempt state gambling laws covering wagering on sports outcomes. This ruling — while only controlling in the Third Circuit — represents the most authoritative pronouncement to date that federal preemption applies to prediction market sports contracts.[^7][^6]

### B. *KalshiEx LLC v. Matthew T. Schuler et al.* — Southern District of Ohio and Sixth Circuit
**Case No.: 2:25-cv-01165 (S.D. Ohio); 26-3196 (6th Cir.)**

On October 7, 2025, Kalshi sued Ohio Casino Control Commission officials in federal court, seeking to enjoin the state from enforcing sports wagering restrictions against its platform. On March 9, 2026, Chief Judge Sarah D. Morrison of the Southern District of Ohio denied Kalshi's motion for a preliminary injunction, ruling that the company's sports-event contracts are not "swaps" under the CEA and that Ohio's gambling laws remain fully enforceable — a ruling directly contrary to the Third Circuit's subsequent holding. Kalshi appealed immediately; the Ohio Casino Control Commission subsequently imposed a proposed $5 million fine. On April 24, 2026, a Sixth Circuit three-judge panel denied Kalshi's emergency motion for injunctive relief pending appeal, stating the preemption arguments were "largely in equipoise (if not favoring Ohio)" at that stage, while ordering Kalshi's appeal to be expedited. The CFTC filed an amicus brief on May 12, 2026, in the Sixth Circuit arguing that the district court took an "improperly narrow view" of the Commission's jurisdiction. Oral arguments in the consolidated Sixth Circuit cases (Ohio and Tennessee) are scheduled for July 30, 2026. A 37-attorney-general coalition — representing states from both parties — filed an amicus supporting Ohio, reflecting the bipartisan nature of the states' position.[^19][^20][^21][^22][^23]

### C. *KalshiEx LLC v. Tennessee Sports Wagering Council*
**Case No.: 3:26-cv-00007 (M.D. Tenn.)**

Tennessee's Sports Wagering Council issued cease-and-desist orders to Kalshi, Polymarket, and Crypto.com on January 10, 2026, threatening civil penalties of $10,000–$25,000 per violation and potential criminal referral. U.S. District Judge Aleta Trauger issued a temporary restraining order on January 13, 2026, blocking Tennessee from enforcing its cease-and-desist. On February 19, 2026, the court granted Kalshi a preliminary injunction, holding that the sports event contracts are likely "swaps" under the CEA and finding it impossible for Kalshi to comply with both state and federal requirements simultaneously. The Sixth Circuit has consolidated the Tennessee and Ohio appeals for oral argument on July 30, 2026.[^24][^25][^26][^27][^19]

### D. *North American Derivatives Exchange, Inc. et al v. State of Nevada* — Ninth Circuit
**Case No.: 26-1304 (9th Cir.); 2:25-cv-00978 (D. Nev.)**

This case, involving Crypto.com's NADEX platform, is the primary vehicle for the Ninth Circuit's consideration of federal preemption. The district court below ruled against the prediction market platforms, consistent with Nevada's position that event contracts constitute gaming under state law. The CFTC filed an amicus brief in the Ninth Circuit asserting its exclusive jurisdiction over event contracts. The Ninth Circuit heard consolidated oral arguments on April 16, 2026, encompassing appeals by Kalshi, Robinhood, and Crypto.com/NADEX — all challenging Nevada Gaming Control Board enforcement. The panel's questioning was notably skeptical of the platforms' preemption arguments: one judge told Robinhood's counsel "I don't buy" the companies' regulatory interpretation and called a Crypto.com argument "sophistry to the nth degree". The panel is expected to rule within 60–120 days of the April 16 argument, meaning a ruling is likely before fall 2026. A Ninth Circuit ruling upholding state authority would directly conflict with the Third Circuit's April 6, 2026 ruling, almost certainly requiring Supreme Court resolution.[^28][^29][^2][^30][^3]

## III. Nevada — Regulatory and Judicial Actions

Nevada was the first state to take formal legal action against prediction market operators and has maintained the most active and aggressive enforcement posture of any gaming jurisdiction in the country.

### A. Nevada Gaming Control Board Cease-and-Desist Against Kalshi (March 4, 2025)

The Nevada Gaming Control Board (NGCB) issued a formal cease-and-desist order to KalshiEx LLC on March 4, 2025, notifying the company that offering event-based contracts on sporting events and election outcomes is "unlawful in Nevada, unless and until approved as licensed gaming by the Nevada Gaming Commission". The order cited multiple Nevada Revised Statutes and Gaming Commission Regulations, set a compliance deadline of March 14, 2025, and warned that past unlawful conduct remained subject to criminal and civil penalties. This was the first formal state cease-and-desist order directed at a CFTC-registered prediction market platform in the country.[^31]

### B. Nevada Federal District Court — Initial Preliminary Injunction for Kalshi (April 2025)

Following the NGCB's cease-and-desist, Kalshi brought suit in federal court. U.S. District Judge Andrew Gordon of the District of Nevada initially granted Kalshi a preliminary injunction in April 2025, blocking the NGCB and Nevada Gaming Commission from enforcing the cease-and-desist while the merits were litigated. This gave Kalshi temporary protection under federal preemption principles at the district court level.[^32]

### C. November 2025 — District Court Reversal and Dissolution of Injunction

In November 2025, Judge Gordon reversed course and dissolved the preliminary injunction, ruling that Kalshi's sports-related event contracts are subject to Nevada's gaming laws. The NGCB's November 2025 notice to industry confirmed the Board's position that "sports event contracts, or certain other event contracts, constitute a wagering activity under NRS 463.0193 and 463.01962".[^33][^34]

### D. Ninth Circuit — Denial of Emergency Stay (February 17, 2026)

Kalshi sought an emergency administrative stay from the Ninth Circuit Court of Appeals to prevent Nevada enforcement pending its appeal. On February 17, 2026, the Ninth Circuit formally denied Kalshi's motion for an administrative stay, clearing the path for Nevada state enforcement. This was a critical procedural step because it effectively allowed the NGCB to proceed in state court without awaiting federal appellate resolution.[^35]

### E. NGCB Civil Enforcement Action Against Polymarket — Carson City District Court (*State of Nevada v. Blockratize, Inc. d/b/a Polymarket*, Carson City District Court, Jan. 16, 2026)

On January 16, 2026, the NGCB filed a civil enforcement action in the First Judicial District Court for Carson City against Blockratize, Inc. d/b/a Polymarket, QCX LLC d/b/a Polymarket US, and Adventure One QSS, Inc. d/b/a Polymarket. The complaint alleged violations of NRS 463.160, NRS 463.350, NRS 465.086, and NRS 465.092. The Board sought a declaration and injunction to stop Polymarket from offering what it characterized as unlicensed wagering to Nevada residents. A temporary restraining order was subsequently granted blocking Polymarket from operating in the state. This was the second state-court civil enforcement action against a prediction market company in the country (after Massachusetts).[^36][^37][^38][^39][^40]

### F. NGCB Civil Enforcement Action Against Kalshi — Carson City District Court (*State ex rel. Nevada Gaming Control Bd. v. KalshiEx, LLC*, No. 26 OC 00050 1B, Nev. Dist. Ct.)

Separately, the NGCB filed a civil enforcement action against Kalshi in Carson City state court, alleging multiple violations of Nevada gaming statutes and seeking declaratory and injunctive relief. Kalshi attempted to remove the case to federal court, arguing federal preemption, but U.S. District Judge Miranda M. Du remanded the case back to Nevada state court on March 2, 2026, ruling that the NGCB's claims arise under state law and that the CEA savings clause preserves state court jurisdiction over gambling-related claims.[^41][^42]

### G. Nevada First District Court — TRO Against Kalshi (March 20, 2026)

On March 20, 2026, Judge Jason D. Woodbury of Nevada's First Judicial District Court granted the NGCB's application for a temporary restraining order against Kalshi. The TRO prohibited Kalshi from offering sports-, election-, and entertainment-related event contracts in Nevada. Judge Woodbury found that Kalshi's contracts fall within Nevada's definitions of a "sports pool" and "percentage game" and therefore constitute unlicensed "gaming" under state law. He concluded that the NGCB had a "strong likelihood of prevailing on the merits" and that harm from unlicensed operations would be "irreparable". The same court had previously issued TROs against Coinbase and Polymarket.[^43][^44][^45][^46]

### H. Additional Nevada Context: Crypto.com/NADEX and Robinhood

The Nevada enforcement actions extended beyond Kalshi and Polymarket. The NGCB secured agreements from Robinhood and Crypto.com to cease offering sports event contracts pending further proceedings, while Kalshi declined to reach a voluntary compliance agreement. The Ninth Circuit consolidated appeals from Kalshi, Robinhood, and Crypto.com/NADEX for the April 16, 2026 oral argument.[^34][^47]

## IV. Massachusetts — Regulatory and Judicial Actions

Massachusetts represents the most procedurally advanced state-level enforcement, having progressed from an administrative warning in November 2025 to a preliminary injunction in January 2026, to appeal before the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court.

### A. Massachusetts Gaming Commission Warning to Sportsbooks (November 13, 2025)

On November 13, 2025, MGC Executive Director Dean Serpa issued a formal notice to the state's seven licensed sportsbook operators — including DraftKings and FanDuel — warning that they are "prohibited from offering sports-related event contracts in Massachusetts, directly or via an affiliate, related business entity, or other association". Licensees were required to submit written responses within 10 business days confirming compliance. Violations could result in penalties up to license revocation under M.G.L. c. 23N. This action followed a similar notice by the Nevada Gaming Control Board the prior month.[^48][^5]

### B. Commonwealth of Massachusetts v. KalshiEX LLC — State Superior Court (*No. 2584CV02525*, Mass. Cmmw. Sept. 12, 2025)

Massachusetts Attorney General Andrea Joy Campbell filed suit against KalshiEx LLC in Suffolk County Superior Court on September 12, 2025 — the first state to file a civil lawsuit against a prediction market company in state court. The complaint alleged that Kalshi's sports-related event contracts — including yes/no positions on Super Bowl winners and March Madness outcomes — constitute sports wagering under Massachusetts law, specifically M.G.L. c. 23N, and that Kalshi had not applied for or received a Massachusetts sports wagering license from the MGC.[^49]

### C. Preliminary Injunction Against Kalshi — Suffolk County Superior Court (January 20, 2026)

Suffolk County Superior Court Judge Christopher Barry-Smith granted the Massachusetts AG's preliminary injunction request on January 20, 2026, against Kalshi. The court denied Kalshi's motion to dismiss, found the state was likely to prove Kalshi required a state license to offer its sports contracts, and held that requiring licensure "serves the public interest". The injunction took effect January 23, 2026, prohibiting Kalshi from accepting online sports wagers from Massachusetts customers. On February 6, 2026, the court rejected Kalshi's request to stay the injunction pending appeal.[^50][^51][^52][^53]

### D. Polymarket v. Massachusetts AG — Federal District Court (*QCX LLC v. Campbell*, D. Mass., No. 1:26-cv-10651, filed Feb. 9, 2026)

Following the Kalshi injunction, Polymarket preemptively sued Massachusetts AG Andrea Campbell in the U.S. District Court for the District of Massachusetts on February 9, 2026, seeking a declaratory judgment of federal preemption and an injunction blocking state enforcement. Polymarket argued that (1) it received CFTC designation as a contract market in July 2025; (2) its contracts qualify as swaps under federal law; and (3) the Kalshi injunction demonstrates Massachusetts's "willingness to use state law to shut down federally authorized markets despite clear federal preemption". The case remains pending in federal district court.[^54][^55][^56]

### E. Commonwealth of Massachusetts v. KalshiEX LLC — Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court (*No. SJC-13906*)

Kalshi appealed the Superior Court preliminary injunction, and the case has been accepted by the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court (SJC) — the state's highest court. The CFTC filed an amicus brief in the SJC on April 23, 2026, arguing that the CEA's comprehensive scheme preempts state law as applied to CFTC-regulated markets. In a striking display of the case's national significance, a coalition of 38 state attorneys general — including New York AG Letitia James — filed a competing amicus brief before the SJC supporting Massachusetts and urging the court to subject Kalshi to state gambling laws. Paradigm (a major crypto investment firm) also filed an amicus brief supporting Kalshi. The SJC's decision will be highly consequential and could either reinforce or conflict with the Third Circuit's ruling.[^57][^58][^59][^60]

## V. CFTC Offensive Litigation Campaign — Six State Lawsuits

In a historically unprecedented regulatory move, the CFTC joined with the Department of Justice to file affirmative federal lawsuits against six states between April 2 and May 19, 2026.

### A. CFTC v. Arizona, Connecticut, and Illinois (April 2, 2026)

The CFTC and DOJ filed three simultaneous federal complaints on April 2, 2026, challenging actions by Arizona, Connecticut, and Illinois against CFTC-registered DCMs. These were the first lawsuits the CFTC has ever filed to prevent state gaming regulators from interfering with prediction market operators. The complaints named governors and state officials in their official capacities and sought (1) declaratory judgments that any state gambling laws are preempted as applied to event contracts on CFTC-registered DCMs, and (2) permanent injunctions preventing enforcement. CFTC Chairman Michael S. Selig stated the agency has "clear and longstanding exclusive jurisdiction to regulate prediction markets". An Arizona federal judge quickly issued a temporary injunction blocking Arizona from enforcing its gambling laws against Kalshi and pausing the state's criminal prosecution.[^61][^62][^63][^64][^65][^16][^1]

### B. CFTC v. New York (April 24, 2026) (*United States v. New York*, S.D.N.Y., No. 1:26-cv-03404)

The CFTC and DOJ sued New York on April 24, 2026, in the Southern District of New York, challenging an October cease-and-desist letter sent to Kalshi and civil enforcement suits filed by AG Letitia James against Coinbase Financial Markets and Gemini Titan. The complaint named Governor Kathy Hochul, AG Letitia James, the New York State Gaming Commission, and senior state officials. The CFTC sought a declaratory judgment of exclusive federal jurisdiction and a permanent injunction.[^66][^67][^68]

### C. CFTC v. Wisconsin (April 28, 2026) (*CFTC v. Wisconsin*, No. 2:26-cv-749, E.D. Wis.)

One week after Wisconsin AG Josh Kaul filed three civil lawsuits in Dane County Circuit Court on April 23, 2026, against Kalshi, Polymarket, Crypto.com, Robinhood, and Coinbase for alleged felony violations of state gambling law, the CFTC countersued Wisconsin in federal court. CFTC Chair Selig stated: "States cannot circumvent the clear directive of Congress. Our message to Wisconsin is the same as to New York, Arizona, and others: if you interfere with the operation of federal law in regulating financial markets, we will sue you".[^69][^70][^71][^72]

### D. CFTC v. Minnesota (May 19, 2026)

After Minnesota Governor Tim Walz signed legislation making Minnesota the first state to criminally ban prediction markets on May 18, 2026, the CFTC and DOJ filed suit the following day in federal district court. The Minnesota law, set to take effect August 1, 2026, makes it a felony to operate, host, manage, or advertise a prediction market platform and even covers VPN services that help users circumvent the ban. The CFTC's complaint described the law as a "flagrant and unprecedented incursion into the Commission's exclusive regulatory sphere" and asked for both preliminary and permanent relief to block the law before it takes effect. The CFTC also filed an emergency motion for a preliminary injunction, warning that allowing the law to take effect would "expose exchanges and their partners to felony prosecutions".[^73][^74][^75][^76]

## VI. State Enforcement Actions (Additional States)

### A. Arizona — Criminal Charges Against Kalshi (March 16–17, 2026)

Arizona Attorney General Kris Mayes filed a 20-count criminal information against KalshiEx LLC and Kalshi Trading LLC in Arizona state court on March 17, 2026 — the first criminal charges filed against any prediction market company in the United States. The charges allege that Kalshi operated an unlicensed wagering business in Arizona in violation of A.R.S. § 13-3301 and accepted bets on professional and college sporting contests, individual player performance, and multiple elections. Notably, four of the 20 counts allege election wagering — including bets on the 2028 presidential race, the 2026 Arizona gubernatorial race, the 2026 Arizona Republican gubernatorial primary, and the 2026 Arizona Secretary of State race — which is separately prohibited under Arizona law regardless of licensure. Misdemeanor convictions could result in asset forfeiture and potential jail time. A federal judge subsequently issued a temporary injunction blocking Arizona's enforcement, including the criminal prosecution, while the CFTC's federal suit proceeds. Kalshi described the charges as "meritless".[^77][^64][^78][^79]

### B. Connecticut — Cease-and-Desist and CFTC Countersuit

Connecticut's Department of Consumer Protection issued a cease-and-desist to Kalshi in December 2025, alleging unlicensed online gambling. The CFTC sued Connecticut on April 2, 2026, as part of its initial three-state complaint action.[^41][^16]

### C. Illinois — Cease-and-Desist and CFTC Countersuit

Illinois sent Polymarket a cease-and-desist in January 2026, characterizing its activity as illegal gambling. The CFTC sued Illinois on April 2, 2026, naming Governor JB Pritzker and state gambling officials.[^80][^36]

### D. Tennessee — Cease-and-Desist to Three Platforms (January 10, 2026)

Tennessee's Sports Wagering Council sent cease-and-desist orders on January 10, 2026, to Kalshi, Polymarket, and Crypto.com, demanding they cease offering sports-linked event contracts to Tennessee residents, void pending contracts, and issue full refunds by January 31, 2026. The SWC warned of civil penalties and criminal referrals for aggravated gambling promotion. Kalshi immediately filed in federal court; Judge Aleta Trauger issued a TRO on January 13 and a preliminary injunction on February 19, 2026.[^25][^27][^81][^24]

### E. Ohio — Regulatory Action, $5 Million Fine, and Pending Sixth Circuit Appeal

Ohio Casino Control Commission issued enforcement orders to Kalshi in 2025, treating sports event contracts as unlicensed sports betting. After losing at the district court level in March 2026 and being denied an emergency stay by the Sixth Circuit in April 2026, Kalshi faces a proposed $5 million fine from Ohio regulators. The consolidated Ohio-Tennessee Sixth Circuit appeal, with CFTC amicus support, is set for oral argument July 30, 2026.[^21][^23][^19]

### F. New York — AG Lawsuits Against Coinbase and Gemini (April 22, 2026)

New York AG Letitia James filed parallel lawsuits in New York Supreme Court in Manhattan against Coinbase Financial Markets, Inc. and Gemini Titan LLC on April 22, 2026, alleging that their prediction market offerings constitute illegal, unlicensed gambling under New York law. The AG alleged both companies allowed users aged 18–20 to participate, below New York's 21-year minimum for mobile sports wagering. The complaints sought forfeiture of illegal profits, restitution to consumers, and civil penalties of up to $100,000 for each instance of unlawfully offering sports wagering. The CFTC sued New York two days later in response.[^67][^82][^83][^84]

### G. Wisconsin — AG Civil Suits and CFTC Countersuit (April 23–28, 2026)

Wisconsin AG Josh Kaul filed three civil lawsuits in Dane County Circuit Court on April 23, 2026, against Kalshi, Polymarket, Crypto.com, Robinhood, and Coinbase, framing the offense as a Class I felony under Wis. Stat. § 945.03(1m). The CFTC countersued Wisconsin in federal court five days later on April 28, 2026.[^70][^71][^72][^69]

### H. Minnesota — First State to Ban Prediction Markets Outright (May 18, 2026)

Governor Tim Walz signed legislation on May 18, 2026, making Minnesota the first state to impose an outright criminal ban on prediction market operations. The law takes effect August 1, 2026, and imposes felony penalties for operating, hosting, managing, or advertising a prediction market. The CFTC and DOJ filed suit the next day seeking to enjoin the law. The Minnesota law's VPN-targeting provision is especially notable from a regulatory-technology standpoint.[^74][^75][^76][^85]

### I. Michigan — Pending Remand Dispute (June 2026)

As of mid-June 2026, Kalshi filed its opposition to Michigan AG Dana Nessel's motion to remand the state's enforcement action to state court, continuing to argue for federal court jurisdiction under the CEA preemption framework. Michigan represents one of the most recently active state enforcement fronts.[^3]

## VII. Federal Criminal and Civil Enforcement — Insider Trading

### *United States v. Van Dyke* / *CFTC v. Gannon Ken Van Dyke* (S.D.N.Y., Apr. 23, 2026)

On April 23, 2026, the SDNY and CFTC announced parallel criminal and civil charges against Gannon Ken Van Dyke, an active-duty U.S. Army Special Forces Master Sergeant stationed in Fayetteville, North Carolina. The charges arise from an alleged scheme in which Van Dyke used classified nonpublic military information about U.S. operations to capture former Venezuelan President Nicolás Maduro ("Operation Absolute Resolve") to trade on Polymarket. Between December 30, 2025, and January 2, 2026, Van Dyke allegedly purchased more than 436,000 "Yes" shares on the "Maduro Out by January 31, 2026?" contract on Polymarket, generating more than $404,000 in profits. The DOJ indictment charges three counts of violating the CEA (each carrying up to 10 years imprisonment), wire fraud (up to 20 years), and an unlawful monetary transaction (up to 10 years). This case carries two historic firsts: (1) the first time the CFTC has charged insider trading on a prediction market; and (2) the first time either the DOJ or CFTC has used the "Eddie Murphy Rule" (CEA provisions prohibiting trading on misappropriated nonpublic government information) in a prediction markets context.[^86][^87][^88][^89]

## VIII. The Influencer/FTC Endorsement Issue (Ifrah Law Article Focus)

The Ifrah Law article's focus — on FTC endorsement disclosure requirements as applied to prediction market influencers — concerns a distinct but legitimate enforcement risk separate from the CFTC/state preemption battle. Politico reported that Polymarket's CMO paid influencer Nick Shirley and 800+ others over $2.5 million during a 14-month span from a personal PayPal account, with at least 490 undisclosed promotions on X. FTC endorsement guidelines (16 C.F.R. Part 255, updated 2023) require "clear and conspicuous" disclosure of "material connections" between influencers and brands, including cash payments. Non-disclosure can trigger FTC enforcement (the Teami precedent involved a $15.2 million judgment, suspended to $1 million based on inability to pay), as well as private class action litigation under state consumer protection statutes and FTC Endorsement Guidelines, with early 2025 class actions against Shein and Celsius estimating damages in the hundreds of millions.[^90][^4]

## X. Emerging Themes and Strategic Observations

**1. The "Swap vs. Wager" Definitional Battle is Everything.** The entire case turns on whether sports event contracts meet the CEA's definition of a "swap" as an instrument with "potential financial, economic, or commercial consequences." Kalshi's argument that downstream effects (merchandise, stadium revenue) qualify is accepted by the Third Circuit and Tennessee; rejected by Ohio; and highly contested at the Ninth Circuit.[^26][^30][^6]

**2. The CFTC's Unprecedented Offensive Posture.** The CFTC's six affirmative state lawsuits in seven weeks (April 2 – May 19, 2026) is, as the Sheppard law firm noted, the agency's most aggressive multi-state litigation campaign in its history. The CFTC is acting as both regulator and litigant, filing direct suits against states and amicus briefs in appellate proceedings simultaneously.[^73][^1]

**3. Bipartisan State Opposition.** While the six states sued by the CFTC all have Democratic attorneys general, eleven of the sixteen total states in proceedings have Democratic AGs and five have Republican AGs. The 38-state AG coalition supporting Massachusetts in the SJC reflects remarkable bipartisan agreement that states retain gambling regulatory authority.[^59][^19][^1]

**4. Nevada's Special Significance.** Given Nevada's status as the "gold standard" of gaming regulation (cited as such in the Massachusetts Gaming Commission's own notice), the Nevada courts' consistent rejection of CFTC preemption arguments carries significant persuasive weight nationally. Nevada's First Judicial District Court issued TROs against Polymarket, Kalshi, and Coinbase — and the Ninth Circuit's oral argument panel appeared skeptical of federal preemption.[^30][^5][^43]

**5. Criminal Risk Is Real.** Arizona's 20-count criminal information against Kalshi and Minnesota's felony-level ban demonstrate that the stakes extend beyond civil regulatory penalties. CFTC Chairman Selig called Arizona's criminal prosecution "entirely inappropriate," but federal courts must still act to block it.[^79][^77]

**6. Supreme Court Resolution Likely Inevitable.** The Third Circuit/Sixth Circuit/Ninth Circuit split, combined with the Minnesota felony ban and the Massachusetts SJC proceeding, makes Supreme Court review highly probable. No circuit has yet affirmed state authority at the appellate level, but the Ninth Circuit — covering the critical Nevada market — appears poised to do so.[^2][^92]
